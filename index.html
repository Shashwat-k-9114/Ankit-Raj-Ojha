Author's Note

I did not set out to become a poet. I set out, at various points, to be an engineer, a rock musician, and — for one serious year — a man who understood spreadsheets. None of it quite took, and somewhere in the failing I ended up reading Kurt Vonnegut for a PhD and writing poems in the margins of the research. I still teach. I still think the funniest line in a poem can carry as much weight as the saddest one, and I have never fully forgiven the people who disagree. This site is where the two halves live together — the scholar footnoting Vonnegut's tricksters, and the man who once got nominated for a prize for a poem about dragons and meant every word of it.

— A.R.O.

Note on the Author

About

ARO

Ankit Raj Ojha writes from Chapra, Bihar, by way of a few other places that shaped him more than he expected. His poems, stories, essays, and criticism have appeared in twenty countries — the Asahi Shimbun in Japan, the San Antonio Review in Uruguay, and a long list of small magazines in between that mattered more to him than either.

He worked briefly as an analyst at Infosys and fronted rock bands in Delhi NCR, Mysore, and Bhubaneswar before either could talk him out of literature. What survived from that decade is a permanent suspicion of anything that takes itself too seriously — including, occasionally, his own PhD. As one early reader of his work put it, his lines flirt with the mirthful even while a mind searching for something steadier moves underneath.

That PhD — in English, from IIT Roorkee — spent several years in the company of Kurt Vonnegut: his tricksters, his myths, his refusal to decide whether the universe is a joke or a tragedy. Ankit is still not sure Vonnegut ever decided either, which is probably the point. He now teaches English with the Department of Higher Education, Haryana, where the same argument continues in front of a classroom instead of a dissertation committee.

Editing has become a second, quieter vocation — as founding editor of The Hooghly Review, consulting editor with Routledge and Springer Nature, and guest editor at Essence and Critique in Turkey. His poem "How to tame your dragons" was nominated for both the Pushcart Prize and Best of the Net, and he placed in the top 1% of the 2023 Wergle Flomp Humour Poetry Contest — an award for bad poetry, on purpose, which he lists here with more pride than the nominations.

The Scholar

Academic Work

16 publications

Ankit's doctoral research at IIT Roorkee spent several years arguing with Kurt Vonnegut — specifically with his tricksters, the myths he half-believed and half-mocked, and his stubborn refusal to say whether human beings are worth saving. The work has appeared in Critique: Studies in Contemporary Fiction, The Explicator, ANQ, Scrutiny2, and Partial Answers, among others. He still isn't sure he's won the argument.
